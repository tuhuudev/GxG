# The Shrimp That Punches Like A Bullet

It looks like a colorful reef creature you could hold in your palm. But the peacock mantis shrimp hides the fastest, most violent punch ever measured in the animal kingdom — a strike so extreme it behaves less like a limb and more like a weapon.

## A Punch as Fast as a Fired Bullet

The mantis shrimp swings its club-like appendages at speeds of around 23 meters per second — roughly 51 miles per hour — underwater, where drag is far greater than in air. The acceleration is even more staggering: about 10,400 g, comparable to the acceleration of a fired bullet. The entire strike is over in less than a thousandth of a second.

## Over 2,500 Times Its Own Body Weight

Each blow delivers more than 1,500 newtons of force — over 340 pounds — from an animal that weighs almost nothing. That is more than 2,500 times the shrimp's own body weight, packed into a limb smaller than a human finger. Pound for pound, nothing else in the animal kingdom hits harder.

## It Boils the Water Around It

The strike is so fast that it rips the water apart. As the club accelerates, it leaves behind a low-pressure pocket that forms tiny vapor bubbles — a process called cavitation. When those bubbles collapse an instant later, they release a shockwave along with a brief flash of heat and light. The prey is hit twice: once by the club, and again by the imploding water itself.

## Strong Enough to Crack Glass

That power has consequences in captivity. Large peacock mantis shrimp have been known to chip and even crack the glass of aquarium tanks. At the Shedd Aquarium, their enclosure is built from acrylic three to four times thicker than a standard exhibit — because ordinary glass simply cannot be trusted to hold. One shrimp, one punch, as fast as a bullet.
