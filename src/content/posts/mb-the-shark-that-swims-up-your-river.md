# The Bull Shark: A Hunter That Leaves the Ocean Behind

Most sharks are bound to the sea. The bull shark is not. It is one of the very few sharks that can thrive in freshwater, and it uses that ability to hunt in the last place anyone expects a shark — far inland, in the rivers where people swim.

## Hundreds of Miles From the Sea

Unlike almost every other large shark, the bull shark can tolerate freshwater for long stretches. It travels far up rivers, and has been recorded roughly 700 miles up the Mississippi River, deep into the American interior. Wherever a large river meets the sea, a bull shark can potentially follow it inland — a shark hunting where the ocean is nowhere in sight.

## A Bite of 1,300 Pounds

That range would be less alarming if the bull shark were not so well armed. Its bite delivers around 1,300 pounds per square inch of crushing force. It is a compact, muscular predator, and that stocky build translates directly into jaw power.

## Pound for Pound, the Strongest Shark Bite

Great white sharks bite harder in absolute terms, but researchers have found that the bull shark has the strongest bite of any shark relative to its body size — the hardest pound-for-pound bite in the shark world. It squeezes an extraordinary amount of force out of a relatively modest frame.

## Nearly Eight Times a Human Bite

Set against the roughly 150 to 160 pounds per square inch a human jaw can produce, the bull shark's bite is close to eight times stronger. Combined with rows of serrated teeth, it is built to seize and hold prey that would tear free of a weaker grip.

## Why It Is So Feared

Power and freshwater tolerance are only part of the story. The bull shark is aggressive and territorial, and it often patrols shallow, murky coastal water and river mouths — precisely the places where people wade and swim. That overlap makes it one of the sharks most often linked to attacks on humans. It is, quite simply, the one shark that can come hunting up your river.
